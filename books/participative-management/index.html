Participative Management

By Yuri Tricys |  Nov 27, 2019  | management-science

Jotted this down today while reflecting on a brief conversation with an oil industry engineer. The conversation got me thinking about the subject of human resource management, which I discovered in 2001, when I first got into business studies. At that time, I discovered Peter Drucker, who became one of my favourite business management writers. I didn't know it at the time, but in his early career he wrote for the Financial Times, one of my all time favourite papers to read. Though I shouldn't profess to have studied their political position, I have read a lot from it and it is one of those publications (like the Economist) that somehow manages to publish a lot of open-minded, left-leaning liberal material, while remaining staunch in its support of free markets and globalization (I support globalization, but not free markets.) In what strikes me as similarly paradoxical, Drucker managed to rise to a highly decorated position within the global elite business culture, while writing consistently about the virtues of what could be called left-leaning, open-minded, organizational business theory. I find it paradoxical because in my experience the majority of business practices, especially in the area of human resource management, are conservative (rather than liberal), which would have made in his time, and probably today, Peter Drucker a minority thinker.

This is my book review section, but here I'm only going to mention rather than review one of Drucker's many books. "The Daily Drucker" is an easy read, that presents a snippet of Drucker's management theories for each day of the year. In his day they were certainly cutting edge, and, in practice they remain so today. After all, management theory is just that, theory. Human Resource management departments remain infamous for pushing paper that gets tucked under the rug, and advanced human resource management theory more often than not, at least from my perspective, only infiltrates a corporate culture when their is a strong leader that not only knows about it, but actively and consistently inundates into the actions of his or her subordinates.

I also want to mention a human resource management text book I found along the way. At some point, I met someone who was dumping a selection of business text-books, and, as devout reader, I took them in and gave them home. "Human Behaviour At Work," written in 1972 was one of them. For a book that was written the year before I was born it's remarkably conscious of many of the issues I've seen played out again and again in my own history of engagement within business organizations. In particular, there is a passage about management styles that spells out, among others, the distinctions between participative and autocratic management styles. I actually transcribed those passages into digital text some years back while trying out my Alpha Smart Neo (a keyboard with only a small LCD screen that still saves text). They're stored in the cloud though, and for now you will have to go on the few words I jotted down this afternoon.


[Autocratic management styles have less information flow from top to bottom and are more 'bossy,' or military in nature. Participative management styles have more information flow from top to bottom and more 'leadership,' where leaders engage as one of rank and file leading by example rather than threat.]



Business, politics, and religion are the 'ideological' subjects. They are rife with conflict because idiology is easily offended and more often than not invasive. To make it work, deal makers typically need to agree with each other and respect each others views.


Aside from climate science and scientology, science is the neutral subject. Conflict doesn't happen on a space station, or typically a study of primates or fossils.


One subject that seems to be largely overlooked in business literature (though there are undoubtedly some papers on it somewhere) is the association between business sectors and management styles. Some sectors are predominately autocratic, like oil and gas, while other sectors are more participative, like journalism, science, and tech.


In the current boom, an elongated global industrial revolution born from the belly of the communications renaissance, there is now visible evidence of progressive change in the distribution of business ideology across sectors. There are delinkages that threaten to break up the monopoly of organizational style on subject matter. This can happen because at scale businesses can integrated horizontally and vertically simultaneously. For example when Branson moves into finance or Facebook into bitcoin. Food for thought.


For example, you could say Microsoft does not have largely an ideological business culture, though their culture is very much known to tilt for market share. They are still more 'sciencey' in effect.
