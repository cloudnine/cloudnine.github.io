Best Geography History Books - Guns, Germs, and Steel - Jared Diamond [DRAFT]

Author: Yuri Tricys |   Apr 6, 2021  |    agriculture, civilization, geography, history

In addition to featuring Jared Diamond's cool voice, the National Geographic film "Guns, Germs, And Steel, The Fate of Human Societies" outlines a theory as relevant to the world we know today as it was to the world before Columbus 'crossed the ocean blue.'

Jared's argument tells us world domination is not a function of racial superiority, but one of geographically enabled knowledge transfer. He argues geographic proximity enabled trade in skills, technology, and biological development, by which he means bacterial and viral immunity.

Of course Jared's award winning books are old news, and I'm not suggesting you run out and buy them, or set your sights on world domination (but if you really want to buy one, e-mail me and I'll send you a copy).

Rather, I want to share the perspective that we are currently today somewhere in the lifespan of history's most influential revolution. Some call it the 'fourth industrial revolution,' others the 'communications revolution.'

I prefer the latter, and I'm convinced it's a renaissance. Which means not only are we are experiencing today conditions that can and likely are increasing the evolution of the human species at speeds exponentially faster than ever before, but also that this evolution will have a net positive progressive outcome.

We can therefore expect in years to come, as an outcome of instantaneous, low barrier global communications, more efficient healthcare, more equitable human rights, better utility yielding international laws, reduced global conflict, and, of course, higher standards of living.

However, achieving these net positive global outcomes is not going to just happen. It's going to happen reflexively, which means because we are going to make it happen. That's going to require some effort on our part as human beings. We can't just sit back and do nothing. We do have to take up the standard of humanitarian progress and carry it forward.

Fortunately, those of us who cannot do the heavy lifting, like creating big time foundations, or working in them, can still make a difference by reaching out, via the technologies our forefathers and peers have provided us, and sharing our views and our knowledge with others.

What Jared taught us is that knowledge transfer improves lifestyle. Now we have most of us access to exponentially higher powered knowledge transfer capacity. We can teach each other, learn from each, and share our knowledge - and this will prompt, and is prompting,the most rapid evolution the human species has ever known. Moreover, because progressiveness drastically improves utility, relative less of it, progressiveness is more likely to flourish in renaissance, which is what we are in.

Remember to think about the positive impact you can have by sharing information, knowledge, and skills with others around the world via today's modern technologies, and to make that happen.

Books are Still a Thing

Don't listen to what anybody says, books are still useful. You can stand on them, use them as coasters. They're cheap, so they make great Christmas gifts, and they look great on your shelves. Even more seriously, there's no better gadget in a bathtub.

Okay, granted it's the twenty-first century, we're busy, and we have tech. You can't spend all day reading, but you should read books everyday. There's still no better way smarten-up, and let's face it, they smell great and they're easy on the eyes.

Now if you're about to head on down to your local bookstore and buy the latest dictionary, don't forget to browse here first. Every now and then we need to write something up and there is nothing sweeter than recollecting well-read treasures.

"I try to make time for reading each night. In addition to the usual newspapers and magazines, I make it a priority to read at least one newsweekly from cover to cover. If I were to read what intrigues me- say, the science and business sections - then I would finish the magazine the same person I was when I started. So I read it all." ~ Bill Gaites
