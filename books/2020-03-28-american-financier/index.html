American Financier, by Jean Strous

Author: Yuri Tricys |  Mar 28, 2020   | non-fiction, learning-center

J.P. Morgan: celebrated, powerfully unambiguous, oligarchically connected, pillar of American history. The man with the bushy furrowed brows, pudgy fat nose, and bulging adams apple. As a school boy, he was infirm, aloof, and disliked. As a business titan his stubby fingers horned on every corner of gilded America.

He built the railway to the west. When Edison dumped Tesla on the street with a shovel - less a year or two of salary - Morgan picked him up and put him back in the money. Who stretched the first underwater cable from Europe to America? You guessed it. Morgan.

The story of J.P. Morgan is the story of American finance, and no one tells it in more detail than Jean Strouse. But let me warn you, she doesn't tell it briefly. It's tome. It's a beast. It's 3 weeks of your life.

I picked up Strouse's biography while wandering the low-level aisles of the Koerner library at the local university, my haunt since childhood. It was late summer of 2010. The students were at the beaches. There was only the occasional creaking and winding of the geared wheeled compacted shelving that accompanied some invisible worm or another in her quest for honours - and the mildly episodic rustling of my own low hanging appendages and baggage when I shifted from my frequently bent position to encourage circulation and change shelves. American Financier was in one of my more frequented spots. I knew the book from a few years back. I'd read several of its neighbours. This was to be the day. I checked it out and checked in to the gilded age.

What distinguishes Strouse, as a historian, from someone like Daniel Yergin (author of 'The Prize') is her wonderful focus on relationships. Every interpersonal nuance is enunciated. Every friendship explored. Every letter written read. The story is more than a timeline, it's a people thing.

Some of my favorite tales are included. The time Morgan and his friends pissed off Morgan's father, also Pierpont, by announcing the departure of a gold-filled ship to England, then turning it around, debarking the gold, and selling at an inflated price. During the gold standard, prices responded quickly to species flow. Morgan Senior was a staunch old-fashioned banker, and old-fashioned banking was about lending not speculation - especially not manipulated speculation.

The beginnings of the family are also explored. The first generation were Calvinists, who emigrated to the new world to found a trading post on a mid-west crossroad. Morgan senior inherited their motel and turned it into a chain, while branching off into the banking business. In those days that was pretty much the coolest thing young money could do. Then Morgan senior moved to England to facilitate the transfer of European old-money into American railway bonds. International banking had been since the Medici's a family affair.

You get the idea. And it goes on. To me it was more than just a history lesson, it was an eye into the world of finance, which at the core is less about efficient trading algorithms and big-O and more about people.

I recommend this read to anyone new to finance, or anyone with the time (and these days discipline) to explore a unique view into the history of the richest empire the world has ever known.

But I warn you, it's a long read, and not the kind of book that gets better when you speed read it and chapter jump. If you're not up for it (and very few people are in this day and age), wait around for a few years. Sooner or later the perfect actor will come along and some peaked movie producer will pick the script out from his discard bin. I'm sure it's been pitched more than a few times.
